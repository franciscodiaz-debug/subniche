# SubNiche Product Overview

## Mission

SubNiche is a niche-first marketplace platform for enthusiast communities to buy, sell, trade, showcase collections, and connect with people who understand their interests.

The product exists because generic marketplaces flatten everything into broad categories. Enthusiasts do not just want listings; they want context, identity, trust, taste, and people who understand the niche.

## Core thesis

A marketplace becomes more valuable when it is organized around identity and community, not merely inventory.

SubNiche should feel less like a generic classifieds site and more like entering a focused enthusiast club where:

- listings are relevant
- people understand the gear
- collections communicate credibility and taste
- trade is normal
- communities can create their own market surfaces
- users can carry one account across multiple niches without collapsing every identity into one generic profile

## Initial wedge

Likely early niches:

- guitars and music gear
- motorcycles / powersports gear
- wine or other enthusiast collectibles

Music gear is a strong likely starting point because it naturally supports:

- collections
- trading
- niche-specific knowledge
- condition nuance
- brand/model obsession
- community discussion
- identity-driven ownership

## Primary user types

### Enthusiast seller

Someone who owns niche items and wants to sell or trade them to people who understand their value.

### Enthusiast buyer

Someone browsing within a niche and looking for relevant, high-signal inventory.

### Collector

Someone who wants to show what they own, track what they want, and connect with others through their collection.

### Trader

Someone who wants to swap items, potentially with cash added on either side.

### Community owner

Someone who operates or participates in a focused group and wants a better marketplace layer for that community.

## Differentiators

### 1. Niche-first marketplace structure

Each niche can have its own categories, attributes, filters, and listing structure. This avoids the generic marketplace problem where everything is treated like broad commodity inventory.

### 2. Trading as native behavior

Trading is not an afterthought. Users can define what they want in exchange and discover real matches instead of posting vague “open to trades” notes.

### 3. Collections as identity and trust context

Collections let users show what they own, what they are into, and what they know. This gives other users passive trust context.

### 4. Community-specific market surfaces

A listing may appear in public market contexts and/or specific community markets. Communities can feel special without isolating the broader platform.

### 5. Profiles with context

Profiles should eventually support niche-specific display, social proof, linked marketplace accounts, verification placeholders, collections, and reputation signals.

## MVP goals

The MVP should prove that SubNiche can support a focused enthusiast marketplace experience with enough depth to feel meaningfully different from Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, Reddit, Discord, or generic marketplace software.

MVP should include:

- account creation
- basic profile
- listing creation
- listing statuses
- marketplace browse
- niche-specific filters
- listing detail page
- collections
- wishlist/wanted items
- trade interests
- basic trade match display
- messaging / offer intent
- community market concept, even if lightweight

## Non-goals for initial MVP

Avoid overbuilding:

- complex payment processing
- full escrow
- full reputation system
- advanced moderation AI
- multi-party trades
- deep analytics
- perfect community governance
- full native mobile app

## Product principles

### Quality over volume

A smaller number of high-quality, relevant listings is better than a large undifferentiated feed.

### Context builds trust

Trust should come from visible context: collections, profile depth, shared niche/community, linked identities, and transaction behavior.

### Specificity beats generality

SubNiche should not feel like “a marketplace for everything.” It should feel like many focused marketplaces sharing one coherent platform.

### Trading must respect explicit user criteria

If a user sets specific trade criteria, the system should not call something a match unless it satisfies those criteria. Lower-confidence suggestions can exist later, but they should not be represented as true matches.

### Preserve community specialness

Communities should feel like distinct spaces with their own market surfaces, while still benefiting from a broader shared platform.

### Keep the UX confident and understated

Avoid generic SaaS copy, gimmicky gamification, or marketplace clutter.
